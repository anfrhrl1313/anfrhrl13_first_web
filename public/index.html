Some of the things you should know as a new tropical fish owner

  1. Ph
    ph is a chemical term that tells you how acidic (or basic) the water is. Each fish species prefers a different ph, and they are extremely sensitive to ph changes. If a fish that normally lives in 6.5ph water is put into water with 7.5ph, they will die pretty soon. You will need a ph meter device, as well as proper sand or soil that helps create such an environment with the appropriate ph level. That's why you need to research about the fish you want in advance and be prepared to spend money.
  2. Temperature
    Temperature is one of the most important factors that determine the survival of your fish. If you have adopted 2 different fish species, and one prefers 18 degrees celsius while the other prefers 26 degrees celsius, that means you cannot have them both in the same fish tank. Also, a thermometer to check the temperature and a heater/cooling system is a must for your fish tank.
  3. Food
    Some fish don't swim much, do not have vision, and rely on their other senses to find food on the ground. If you have this type of fish as well as other, more active fish swimming up higher, these active fish will "steal" all the food that sits at the top of the tank before it can sink down, and the "eyeless" fish will eventually starve. What you can do is get a type of food that sinks to the ground quickly and turn off the light-- the bottom-dwelling fish are normally active at night and will find the food that other fish that rely on vision can't.
  4. Air circulation
    You need some type of system that creates water flow inside the tank, especially near the bottom if you have fish that normally spend time on the floor level. Air circulation inside the tank is important for two reasons. One, it helps water flow into the filters. Two, it creates water surface wiggle, so more oxygen can be absorbed into the water.
  5. Filters
    Filters are a must. The size and number of filters depends on how big your tank is and how many fish you have. Having filters (especially a sponge type) is crucial because good bacteria that improve water quality reside in the filters.
  6. The first time
    Let's say you've just bought a fish. You have a perfectly set-up fish tank for your new fish. You cannot just put him/her in your tank right away. Fish are small and are subject to getting shocked and stressed by abrupt changes in their environment. They are likely to die if you change their environment suddenly. What you might want to do is put whatever vessel your fish is in (normally a bag with fish, oxygen and water) inside the prepared fish tank. Leave it for 30 minutes to an hour, and then let them dive. This helps them slowly adjust to the new temperature.
  7. 30% Rule
    Even if you have a perfect cycling system in your fishtank, and the water quality seems okay, you have to clean your tank every now and then but not too often. Maybe once a week, take out 30 percent of the water, dump it, and fill the tank back up slowly. There are tools for the process that you need to purchase. Also,the new water you add to the tank has to be conditioned first. Get a solution called PSB ("photosynthetic bacteria"). You will have to add it to the new water that will be added to the tank roughly 30 minutes prior to incorporating it into the tank. The solution helps stabilize tap or other impure water by creating an appropriate ph level.
